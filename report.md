# Environmental Analysis Report: Aquifer Preservation and Ecosystem Health

The primary objective of this project is to monitor and predict water quality indicators to prevent ecosystem degradation. By leveraging predictive modeling, we aim to preserve local water bodies and aquifers from reaching critical contamination levels. Initially, we explored a business case for farmer participation—specifically, encouraging declarations of product usage. However, this has proven challenging because the nitrates used in agriculture often migrate into deep aquifers that farmers do not utilize for their crops, meaning there are no immediate negative consequences for their harvest. For the city of Nancy, the impact on tap water is also mitigated as the primary source is the Moselle, unlike other regions such as the Alsace Water Table, the Champagne Chalk Aquifer, and the Lorraine Plateau Aquifer, where agricultural proximity has led to significant contamination and abandonment of water sources.

Consequently, the core business case has shifted from agricultural productivity to a pure ecosystem preservation model, functioning independently of farmer participation. In the Nancy region, we observe a high correlation between rainfall and nitrate levels. This is largely attributed to the replacement of natural filters, such as wild fields and wetlands, with industrial agricultural fields. This transformation causes runoff to carry pollutants directly into the drainage network and eventually into the Moselle. A major milestone for the Grand Nancy area is to make the Meurthe river suitable for swimming by 2028. Achieving this requires addressing the threat of eutrophication caused by nitrates, which fuels algae proliferation. As these algae die, bacteria consume vast amounts of dissolved oxygen during decomposition, leading to hypoxia and the localized death of aquatic fauna and flora. Furthermore, high nitrate concentrations contribute to soil erosion.

The chemical balance of the soil is further compromised by the use of de-icing salts. Sodium ions from salt displace essential nutrients like calcium and magnesium within the clay-humus complex. This displacement triggers clay dispersion, leading to soil compaction, loss of air and water permeability, and a significant increase in pH (alkalization). These structural changes in the soil also directly impact regional ecology; sodium breaks down the soil's organic structure, releasing humus and organic compounds that were previously sequestered.

Finally, we observe a strong correlation between de-icing salt usage and spikes in Colored Dissolved Organic Matter (CDOM). Salt not only degrades the soil structure but also accumulates in fallen vegetation. During snowmelt, the resulting runoff leaches these degraded soils and decomposing organic matter into the river system. This influx of organic acids and tannins causes CDOM levels to climb, further straining the aquatic environment. In summary, monitoring markers like phycocyanine (toxic algae) and Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD/DBO) remains critical to preventing the total asphyxiation of local aquatic ecosystems.
